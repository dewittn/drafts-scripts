Overview

What is your approach to designing websites for mission-driven organizations with small teams?

After an initial discovery period where we work with our clients to identify their requirements, we create a prototype design to make sure we are in agreement with the look and layout of the new site. As we build out the site we share a preview version, where clients can view the work in progress and provide feedback along the way. We believe that feedback is a vital part of the process, especially when working with small teams because an organization’s online presence plays such a critical role in its marketing message.

What tools and plug-ins would you recommend to help automate workflows and create a frictionless user experience?

When it comes to building automated workflows we recommend Zapier. This service acts as a bridge that can connect many online sites and platforms. For example, Zapier could be used to add people who donate through Donately directly to the membership backend and even subscribe them to a newsletter. There are several other plugins and services that could be used to build a better user experience for the members of USLAF, which we would be happy to discuss in more detail.

As an alternative to WordPress, we recommend Ghost Blog, which is geared toward publishing but has membership and newsletter functionality built in and does not require the use of extensive third-party plugins. This not only makes Ghost easier to manage but also creates a smoother experience for members and subscribers.

What experience do you have integrating best practices concerning accessibility for web content into web design projects?

When working on a design we try, as much as possible, to include accessible features such as responsive text, light and dark color variants, landmark regions, and aria labels, which are important for screen readers and voice-over controls. We recognize that accessibility on the web is an ever-evolving field and we try to keep up with best practices like those laid out by organizations such as The A11Y Project. Our primary goal when designing sites making them accessible by the widest range of devices and screen sizes possible.

Design & Accessability

We are of course happy to discuss the option of designing a custom WordPress theme for this project but based on previous experience it is often more trouble than it is worth.

As for the design of the new site, we would like to focus on telling the story of USLAF, while highlighting the great work that its members are doing. This would not only help address accessibility issues but also make USLAF’s mission easier to understand.

Technical Recommendations

When it comes to integrating WordPress with your donation platform, Donately, it is pretty easy to embed the donation form into one of the pages. Managing the members, however can be a little more challenging.

Generally speaking, when it comes to WordPress we recommend using as few plugins as possible. For this project, that might mean using services like Memberful to handle USLAF memberships rather than a plugin like Memberpress. While there is nothing inherently wrong with plugins, they add more complexity that can slow down the site and make it more susceptible to hacking.

When it comes to making the user experience more seamless, Zapier is a fantastic tool that can automate a lot of this worm. It can handle importing the contact information from people who donate on Donately, and bring them into WordPress, Memberful, or Ghost. It’s important to note that a lot of the technical decisions depend heavily on what services USLAF wishes to provide to its members. While we can make some recommendations here, a conversation would these recommendations a lot more concrete.

There are of a wide range of other technologies that could be used to do this project, such as keeping the site on Squarespace while improving it with design and integration enhancements. The technology decisions ultimately depend on two important questions, what is the experience USLAF wants to create for its members, and what services does it wish to provide.

Timeline

Our proposed timeline is to spend a few weeks on discovery, where Coto.Studio would work with USLAF to nail down all design, platform, and integration questions. We would then spend the fall, September through November migrating the existing content and working on the site design. From December to March we would focus on integration and automation, with the goal of finishing work by early March so that there would be time to test and check everything before the site’s launch. Given that the existing site is not terribly complex, it’s probable that the work could proceed faster than this proposed timeline.

Risks and Challenges

We believe there will be two challenges to completing this project on time. One is the platform and services integration. The other will be customizing the theme.

One of the biggest risks would be choosing the wrong technology stack. For example, we could start developing the site with WordPress only to find it won’t do everything we need it to. No technology is ever a perfect fit and always involves trade-offs. One of the best ways to minimize the risk of choosing the wrong technology stack is by having an adequate discovery period where we can make sure it meets the needs of the people who will use it the most.
